## Problem Statement

MBA and professional candidates preparing for MBB consulting interviews (McKinsey, BCG,
Bain) have no reliable way to know if they are actually improving. Progress tracking is
fragmented across spreadsheets and ad hoc notes. Peer feedback is inconsistent and too
vague to act on. Existing platforms (CaseCoach, RocketBlocks, PrepLounge) provide drills
and peer matching but no cross-session intelligence — no quantified measurement of skill
progression over time.

## What Makes This Different

The original PRD positioned PrepSignal as an "analytics layer" sitting on top of
CaseCoach and RocketBlocks via API. This design rejects that framing.

**Core insight:** An analytics layer on top of other platforms is a weak position. You
depend entirely on their API access, roadmap decisions, and willingness to let a
competitor sit between them and their users. If they pull the integration, the product
collapses.

**New framing:** PrepSignal owns the mock session itself. Candidates use PrepSignal to
log, upload, or paste in session data — and the AI scores it immediately. No third-party
integration needed. First-party data from day one.

## Constraints

- 3-person team (2 PMs: Abhishek & Zoya; 1 Engineer: Ryan)
- MBA class project timeline (estimated 6–10 weeks)
- Web-only (no mobile app in v1)
- No third-party API partnerships assumed (CaseCoach, RocketBlocks dropped from scope)
- Validation study required before launch: calibrate LLM scores against human expert ratings

## Glossary

- **Validation study**: the pre-launch activity where human raters score sample transcripts and their scores are compared to the LLM's scores. Used throughout this document consistently.

## Premises

1. The core pain is measurement — candidates can't tell if they're improving. AGREED.
2. LLM scoring is NOT assumed to be reliable out of the box — it requires calibration
   against human expert raters before the dashboard numbers mean anything. CHALLENGED.
3. Third-party API partnerships (CaseCoach, RocketBlocks) are NOT feasible on a class
   timeline and are cut from scope. CHALLENGED (and cut).
4. Recurring demand within a prep cycle (4–16 weeks, 2–5 sessions/week) is sufficient
   for the class project. Long-term subscription economics are a future problem. AGREED.
5. The social layer (streaks, study groups, scheduling) is deferred — the AI scoring
   engine is the hard bet; prove it first. AGREED.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: The Feedback Formatter (Minimal Viable)
Upload a transcript → get a structured rubric. No progression tracking, no dashboard.
Effort: S | Risk: Low
Best for: Proving the LLM scoring claim quickly.
Cut: Everything except the scoring engine.

### Approach B: Full Vision (as written in PRD)
AI scoring + dashboard + social layer + integrations + mobile app.
Effort: XL | Risk: High
Problem: Integration partnerships unlikely on class timeline. LLM validity unproven.
Social layer and AI engine are separate hard problems. Too much scope for 3 people.

### Approach C: Score the Session (RECOMMENDED)
PrepSignal owns the session log. Candidates submit sessions directly to PrepSignal.
AI scores immediately. Dashboard shows progression over time. Peer feedback rubric
is the output of an in-platform session, not a form collected elsewhere.
Effort: M | Risk: Medium
Cut: Third-party integrations, mobile app, social gamification (streaks, study groups).
Keep: AI scoring engine, skill radar, time-series dashboard, one-priority recommendation.

## Recommended Approach

**Approach C: Score the Session.**

### Auth Strategy (decided)
- First session: anonymous — no account required. User pastes text, gets a feedback card.
- Progression tracking (radar history, time-series graph): requires account creation.
- This lowers acquisition friction while preserving the data model for returning users.
- **Anonymous → account handoff:** After the anonymous feedback card is displayed, surface
  a "Save your progress" CTA. On account creation, link the anonymous session to the new
  user: `UPDATE sessions SET user_id = $new_user_id WHERE session_id = $cookie_session_id`.
  Store the anonymous session_id in a browser cookie immediately on submission so it
  survives to the account creation step.

### Inputs (three modes, user's choice)
1. **Paste notes** — free-text field, ≥ 200 words required (see Session Minimum below), no account needed to try
2. **Upload transcript** — .txt accepted; .pdf accepted via pdf-parse library (text-based PDFs only — scanned/image PDFs explicitly out of scope for v1, surface a clear error message)
3. **Structured log** — guided form with the following fields:

| Field | Type | Required |
|-------|------|----------|
| Case type | Dropdown (Profitability / Market Sizing / M&A / Operations / Growth / Other) | Yes |
| Duration (minutes) | Number | Yes |
| Your structure opening | Textarea | Yes |
| Key quantitative work done | Textarea | No |
| Recommendation given | Textarea | Yes |
| Partner feedback (free text) | Textarea | No |

**Session minimum:** ≥ 200 words of combined input content required. If below threshold, block submission with message: "Add more detail about your session — we need at least 200 words to generate a reliable score."

### AI Processing
LLM evaluates session content and scores across 6 dimensions:
- Structuring
- Quantitative rigor
- Creativity
- Synthesis
- Communication
- Business judgment

Each dimension gets: a score (1–5) + a specific quoted excerpt from the session that
justifies the score. The quote is mandatory — it is what makes feedback actionable
rather than generic.

### Outputs
1. **Immediate feedback card** — scores on all 6 dimensions with quoted justifications.
   Example: "Synthesis: 2/5 — When asked for a recommendation, you said 'there are a
   few options to consider' rather than committing to one."

2. **Skill radar chart** — hexagon visualization of the session's shape across 6
   dimensions. Weak in quant, strong in structure — visible at a glance.

3. **Progression graph** — time-series line per dimension across all sessions. The
   "aha" moment: "My structuring went from 2 → 4 over 6 sessions but synthesis has
   been flat the whole time."

4. **One priority recommendation** — not a list of 6 things. One. "Based on your last
   3 sessions, synthesis is your most persistent gap. Focus here first."
   Algorithm: select the dimension with the lowest rolling average score across the
   user's most recent 3 sessions. Ties broken by the dimension showing the least
   improvement (smallest score delta from session 1 to session 3). Requires ≥ 2
   sessions; do not show this card on the first session.

### Critical Pre-Launch Step: Validation Study
Before any user sees a score, run a validation study:
- Collect 5 sample transcripts spanning a range of performance quality
- Target: minimum 5 raters, ideally 10. Fewer is better than no study.
- Rater sources (in order of preference):
  1. Classmates with recent MBB offers — easiest, free
  2. MBA career center advisors — free, accessible, have done hundreds of mocks
  3. Paid case coaches — 2–3 sessions, ~$500, former MBB consultants
  4. Proxy validation: compare offer-holders vs. non-offer-holders on score distributions
  (Note: Victor Cheng's LOMS or similar commercial products require rights verification
  before use as ground truth — do not use without checking licensing terms)
- Ask each rater to score each transcript 1–5 per dimension (30 data points per rater per transcript)
- Timeline: recruit and collect in 1–2 weeks, not "this week" — this is a real commitment
- Compare rater scores to LLM scores across all 6 dimensions
- **Agreement definition:** LLM score is within ±1 of the median rater score for that
  dimension. (Exact match on a 1–5 scale is too strict; ±1 is the conventional threshold.)
- Report agreement rate in the demo: "Our model agreed with experienced evaluators on
  [X]% of dimension scores across 5 sessions"
- Threshold: 75%+ agreement = proceed. Below 75% = iterate on the scoring prompt.
  Do not show any user a dashboard until this threshold is met.
- **Prompt engineering is a first-class build artifact.** Budget 2–3 revision cycles
  during the validation study. The scoring prompt is not a one-shot draft — it is the
  core product. Track which dimensions disagree most and revise those prompt sections first.

## Error Handling

- **LLM timeout (> 30s for short inputs, > 60s for long inputs):** Surface error to user, do not store partial session. Message: "Scoring took too long — try again or paste a shorter version."
- **LLM returns score outside 1–5:** Clamp to range, log for monitoring.
- **LLM returns no verbatim quote for a dimension:** Retry once with a stricter prompt. If second attempt also fails, store session with a placeholder ("No specific quote extracted") and flag for review. Do not block the user.
- **PDF parsing fails (scanned/image PDF):** Reject with message: "We can't read image-based PDFs yet — paste your transcript as text instead."
- **Input below 200-word minimum:** Block at submission, do not call LLM.

**LLM latency note:** For long transcripts (thousands of words), LLM scoring across 6 dimensions may exceed 30 seconds. Mitigations: stream the response to show partial results as they arrive; set a user-visible "Analyzing your session..." state; consider a lighter/faster model for initial scoring. Validate latency during development and adjust the success criterion if needed.

## Build Priority (cuts if time-constrained)

Build in this order. Cut from the bottom if the timeline runs short.

1. Paste input → LLM scoring → feedback card (the core loop)
2. Validation study (before showing scores to real users)
3. Account creation + session storage
4. Skill radar chart (single session)
5. Progression time-series graph (multi-session)
6. One-priority recommendation (requires ≥ 2 sessions)
7. Upload (.txt first, then .pdf)
8. Structured log form (last — highest complexity, lowest uniqueness)

**If time runs short for demo:** drop items 7–8 entirely. Use paste-only with static test data for the progression graph. A working paste → score → radar chart is a complete demo.

## Open Questions

1. **Scoring prompt design** — What does the LLM prompt look like for each dimension?
   Require the model to output structured JSON with `score` (int 1–5) and `quote`
   (verbatim string from input). Reject/retry if format is malformed.

2. **Dimension weighting** — Are all 6 dimensions equally important for MBB outcomes?
   The validation study may reveal that structuring and synthesis matter more. Treat
   as equal weights for v1; revisit after validation data is in.

3. **Chart library** — Recommend Recharts (React-native, good radar support). Single
   session shows one radar polygon. Multi-session overlays or animates latest vs. prior.
   Empty state (0 sessions): show greyed-out radar with "Submit your first session to
   see your shape."
   **Progression graph states:**
   - 0 sessions: hide the graph entirely, show "Complete 2 sessions to see your trends."
   - 1 session: still hidden — same message.
   - 2+ sessions: render time-series lines per dimension. X-axis = session number,
     Y-axis = score (1–5). One line per dimension, color-coded.

4. **Data privacy** — Session transcripts may contain confidential case content from
   coaching services. Add a clear data handling policy before launch. At minimum: state
   whether transcripts are used for model training and give users a delete option.

## The Assignment

Before writing any code: run the validation study. Find 5–10 classmates with MBB offers,
give them 5 sample transcripts, ask them to score each dimension 1–5. Run
the same transcripts through your LLM scoring prompt. Compare. If you hit 75%+
agreement, you have a credible product. If you don't, you know what to fix in the
prompt before you build the dashboard around it.

The validation study is the first engineering task, not the last.
